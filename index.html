What is Tabata?

A 4-Minute Protocol with Maximal Effort

A Tabata is a high-intensity workout protocol that was invented in 1996 by Dr. Izumi Tabata, a Japanese physician and researcher. He scientifically proved that a 4 minute high-intensity workout was more beneficial than 60 minutes of moderate exercise. He conducted a study between two groups of athletes using an interval-based training model.

One group was subjected to 20 seconds of maximal effort bicycling followed by 10 seconds of rest, *Repeated eight rounds for a total of four minutes. The other control group did 60 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise. Both groups worked out for five days a week for a grand total of five hours a week or 20 minutes.

The protocol lasted for 6 weeks and the results showed that the Tabata group improved both its aerobic and anaerobic fitness levels with the anaerobic fitness level increasing 28%. Due to very high intensity, heart rate and metabolism jumped up, which is imperative for burning calories 12 hours after exercise.

How Do I Get Started?
Before any strenuous activity it is important to take 10 minutes to warm up with dynamic stretching. This prevents injury, increases performance, and assists in recovery. With any new method of training it is always best to work into it gradually, especially one that entails high-intensity. Do not ever sacrifice form, which can easily occur due to fatigue.

Maximal-effort, sprint-like activities are the key component for Tabata workouts. Intensity, not duration, is the focus. This is geared for conditioning rather than strength training. Optimal exercises would typically be: sprinting, hill sprinting, stair sprinting, bicycling, rowing, swimming, jumping rope, sled pushing.

Follow a provided TRAINING PROGRAM or choose between 1-4 exercises, prepare your training area, and use this TABATA TIMER to get started. Push yourself with maximal effort for four minutes using the 20 second work and 10 second rest interval ratio.
